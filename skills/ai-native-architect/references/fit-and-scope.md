# Fit & Scope — who this is for, who it is not, and the two tracks

The hardest and most valuable thing this skill does is **refuse the wrong job honestly**. The user
may arrive wanting "AI transformation" of a traditional company. If you relabel that as AI-Native
you have failed them — you've sold a graft as a rebuild. Use this file to classify the request and,
when out of scope, to say so in the methodology's own terms.

## The classification (Step 0 in full)

### TRACK A — Greenfield (the home case)
A new venture, startup, new organization, new product line, or a new unit built **from zero**.
There is no legacy structure to fight. Run the full procedure generatively: state T1 for the org,
design judgment distribution × context flow, redraw the workflow graph, specify the four layers,
and — critically — **do not import the 16 bottlenecks**. The main failure mode here is
*over-engineering*: a 3-person company does not need a knowledge graph and six observability tools
on day one. Match substrate weight to the unit of coherence.

### TRACK B — Brownfield carve-out (the only valid incumbent path)
An incumbent that will **win a patch of independent ground and build a new unit from scratch** under
this methodology, run it alongside the legacy org, and let the contrast in output drive broader
change. This is in scope. Apply the **full procedure to the new unit**; the legacy org appears only
as the **diagnostic baseline** (Step 2) that explains *why* a carve-out, not a graft, is required.
Do not design a plan to "convert" the legacy org — that is not this methodology.

**Keep the carve-out genuinely independent — or it quietly becomes change-management.** The unit
must be *its own accountable thing*: its own outcome metric / P&L line, its own service contract to
whoever it serves, and the freedom to win or lose on output. The mechanism that drives broader
change is **competitive contrast** (legacy units see the numbers and follow, or keep losing), not
persuasion or a top-down mandate. A "shared-services layer that everyone adopts" is centralization
dressed as a carve-out; if you can't point to what makes the new unit independently accountable,
you've slid back into transforming the old graph. (Watch this especially when the new unit serves
the legacy units — centralizing a back office can also erode the *local* relationships that were the
moat; design so the unit captures and renews that relationship capital, not just absorbs it.)

### OUT OF SCOPE — AI-enablement dressed up as AI-Native
Signals: "transform the whole company," "make our existing teams faster with AI," "roll out
copilots across departments," "keep our org chart but add an AI layer," "phase an AI rollout."
This is **AI-enabled, not AI-Native** — a difference of *kind*, not degree (see the SKILL.md table).
You cannot graft AI onto a pre-AI architecture and get a native org. **Do not produce a blueprint as
if you could.** Instead, deliver the honest verdict and the one real path. Verbatim framing to adapt:

> **This is AI-enablement, not AI-Native — and that's a difference in kind, not degree.** Plugging
> ChatGPT/Cursor/copilots into your existing workflows makes your organization *AI-enabled*. It will
> speed up individual nodes, but your organization's speed is set by its *graph*, and the graph is
> unchanged — so end-to-end throughput barely moves (the documented pattern: ~95% of custom
> enterprise AI pilots show no measurable P&L impact in ~6 months). **You are not the target group
> of this methodology in its unmodified form**, and I won't pretend a rebuild is possible by
> grafting.
>
> There are two honest options:
> 1. **If you want the efficiency gains of AI-enablement** — that's a legitimate goal, but it's the
>    *adjacent* methodology: phased decomposition, change management, and protected greenfield zones.
>    Pursue it as enablement, with realistic (node-level, not graph-level) expectations.
> 2. **If you actually want AI-Native** — the only structure that delivers it inside an incumbent is
>    to **carve out an independent new unit and build it from zero** under this methodology (Track B),
>    then let its output stand in contrast to the legacy units and let that contrast drive change.
>    I can design *that* with you right now.

Then, if they choose option 2, re-enter as **Track B**.

**Gating honestly is not an excuse to ship a thin memo.** Even when you correctly stop short of a full
blueprint, *price the fork*: size the opportunity from the org's own numbers (e.g. a stated 6% linen
loss on $34M revenue ≈ $2M/yr recoverable) and attach an order-of-magnitude cost band + the single key
ROI metric to *each* option, so "which do you want?" is a decision the operator can actually price
(depth module 1 in `depth-checklists.md`). A reviewer who asked "where do we start?" and got a
principled refusal with zero numbers received half an answer.

### BOUNDARY — emotional-labor-led domains
Domains where the core of the value is deep human emotional labor — **deep psychological
counseling, end-of-life / hospice care, the core relationship of childhood education**. Here
**AI-Native can assist but must not lead.** Design the model/agent/context/observability layers to
*support* the human at the center (logistics, knowledge, drafting, follow-up), and explicitly keep
agents out of the relationship's core. Flag this in the Scope Verdict rather than over-claiming a
fully agentic design. Other strong-trust, high-stakes-judgment domains (clinical decisions, legal
liability, safety-critical control) keep more human judgment anchors and heavier policy gates —
they are *in scope* but with a different judgment/context balance, not a thinner one.

## Why greenfield and brownfield are different *in logic*, not just degree

| | Greenfield (Track A) | Brownfield (Track B) |
|---|---|---|
| **Starting point** | a theorem (T1) and a blank page | a legacy structure that *is* the 16 bottlenecks |
| **Core move** | generate the graph; never import bottlenecks | carve out a new unit; **do not** transform the old one |
| **Role of the diagnostic** | inverted into design rules | the *argument* that in-place fixing is impossible |
| **Main failure mode** | over-engineering the substrate too early | smuggling legacy roles/hand-offs into the "new" unit; or sliding into a change-management plan |
| **What "done" looks like** | a from-zero native architecture | a from-zero native *unit* + the contrast strategy vs. legacy |

The deep reason they differ: **AI-Native is achieved by what you *don't* build, not only by what you
do.** Greenfield's advantage is purely negative freedom (no bottlenecks to import). Brownfield can't
get that freedom by editing the existing graph — editing preserves the topology that causes the
bottlenecks — so it must *spawn a new graph* and let it compete. A plan that edits the old graph is,
by definition, the adjacent methodology. Recognizing this is the point of the gate.

## A note on the user's likely framing
Users often say "我们要做 AI 转型 / AI transformation." Take it as an honest starting signal, not a
spec. Your job is to find out whether they want enablement (efficiency on the existing graph) or
native (a redrawn graph), name the two clearly, and design only the one they actually choose. Doing
this well — early, kindly, and without false promises — is itself the methodology in action.
