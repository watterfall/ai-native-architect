# The dashboard, the metacognitive loop, and the evidence ledger

Two jobs here: (1) build a **progress dashboard (仪表盘)** that keeps the learner's self-read honest, and
(2) hold the **evidence discipline** so the protocol warns without overclaiming. Both exist because the
central failure on this surface is metacognitive, not motivational.

## The metacognitive trap the dashboard exists to catch

**Recognition fluency is misread by the brain as reproduction capacity.** You read along with the
agent's derivation, "recognize" each step smoothly, conclude you learned it — then can't reproduce it
three days later. *Recognition* ("seeing the right answer and recognizing it as right") is cheap, fast,
and AI pushes it to the extreme. *Reproduction* ("generating it from scratch, no prompt") is expensive,
lagged, uncompensable by any tool — it requires a path already in the learner's head. This is the
"knowing-doing" cost gap (FIG L.0) as it appears in *subjective experience*. **The dashboard's whole
purpose is to force the test from recognition to reproduction**, because only the reproduction gate
separates real capability from the false "looks like I can."

## The dashboard — leading indicators (measure capability, not throughput)

Never track "amount looked up" or "answer-recall rate" — those measure execution, not learning. Track:

- **Transfer-test pass rate** — can the learner perform in a *new* context with **no agent present**? If
  they perform well only when AI is present, what grew is *dependence*, not capability. A harsher
  version: with AI closed and a 3-day gap, can they **regenerate** (not merely recognize) the
  derivation? Only then is compilation complete.
- **Challenge hit-rate** — how often, and how *correctly*, the learner pushes back on AI outputs. A
  healthy AI-native learner *increasingly* pushes back, and pushes back **correctly** ([R18] is the
  operable calibration quantity). Note: challenge hit-rate is **in-domain error-detection sensitivity** —
  it rides on the "something's off here" intuition that only the in-domain doing loop deposits. It
  **cannot** be conjured by a standalone "critical thinking course" — a senior physician smells a dubious
  diagnosis instantly but is just as fooled by AI fluency in an unfamiliar legal document.
- **Reflection-log return-use rate** — does the learner actually go back and reread their own errors? (a
  store never reread doesn't compound).
- **Removal-drill result** — pull the AI today on the core task: output merely *dips* (still on the
  scaffold side) vs. *collapses* (crossed into crutch, FIG L.9). This is the only act that *locates the
  break-point*, which is never printed on the tool.
- **Friction habituation** — has "adding friction on purpose" shifted from a willpower act to a process
  default? (the willpower-to-process migration, observable).

## The metacognitive monitoring loop (FIG L.10) and where AI corrupts it

Whether the learner stays on the scaffold side depends on a three-node loop running true: **① self-assess
"do I really know it?" → ② calibrate that feeling against one *real test* (not the fluency of rereading)
→ ③ run desirable-difficulty practice on the gap → back to ①.** The loop's weak point is **node ①**: AI
keeps injecting **synthetic confidence**. Name the gauge in the dashboard:

- **[R19] METR's "felt faster, measured slower"** — the canonical offloading-evidence finding: people
  *believe* they are faster/on the scaffold side while measurement shows otherwise. It is the warning
  that the learner thinks they're on the left of the break-point while already past it.

When the challenge hit-rate (node ②'s calibration signal) falls, node ① is corrupted and the loop spins
empty — the dashboard should flag this as the primary alarm.

## The evidence ledger — grade B: a warning plus a falsifiable bet

**State the stance exactly as the methodology does, and do not drift off it: this is grade B — an
evidence-grounded warning plus a falsifiable bet, NOT "AI makes you stupid."** Writing atrophy as
established fact overshoots the evidence and backfires on the protocol's credibility. Keep claims and
evidence unmixed (`../../../references/_core/canon-vocab.md` evidence discipline). Both sides on the table:

**Concern side (correlational / short-term, to be substantiated):**
- Gerlich 2025 (Societies 15(1):6, N=666, **cross-sectional correlational**): AI use negatively
  correlates with critical thinking, mediated by cognitive offloading (total effect b≈−0.42); the author
  concedes no causation, no longitudinal data.
- MIT/Kosmyna 2025 (arXiv preprint, **un-reviewed**, N=54 → 18 by session 4): EEG "cognitive debt" —
  tiny sample.
- Mechanistic precedent: the **Google effect** (Sparrow 2011) — when re-access is expected, people
  recall the info less and recall *where to find it* more. (Note: has **failed to replicate robustly** —
  cite as mechanistic hint, not verdict.) Likewise GPS-hippocampus is correlational with a direction
  problem; the calculator-arithmetic claim lacks a strong first-hand anchor — **mechanistic plausibility,
  not causal proof about AI.**

**Counter side (must be shown, not buried):**
- The strongest *causal* studies (scaffolded AI tutoring) show **+0.73 to +1.3 SD** — used well, AI
  lifts learning significantly.
- The **redistribution hypothesis** (2026 review): AI may *reallocate* cognitive resources from
  offloadable low-order tasks toward evaluation / synthesis / metacognition; existing studies conflate
  "less effort on offloaded tasks" with "generalized impairment." The extended-mind thesis points the
  same way — offloading is the cognitive boundary moving outward, not capacity draining.

**The hinge:** a structured-prompting RCT (MDPI Data 2025, n=150) — *unguided* AI use fosters offloading
without improving reasoning; *structured* use significantly reduces offloading and improves reasoning and
reflection. The effect is **not fixed; it depends on how you use it** — which is the operable foothold
for the whole protocol.

## Why resisting convenience is insurance, not a moral claim

Frame it as **decision theory, not virtue.** In the window where atrophy and redistribution are
undecided, the *expected cost* of resisting (reorder, keep a struggle window, a few minutes a day) is
very low; the *potential loss* hedged is very large (if atrophy is real, the loss is judgment
sovereignty — not recoverable after the fact). The resistance postures (self-answer first, keep the
loop, build desirable difficulty) **lose in neither world**: if atrophy is true they avert it; if
redistribution is true they are *exactly* the moves that steer resources upward. A move that is
low-cost, highly protective against the worst case, and not even losing in the best case is, in any
rational framework, insurance worth buying. Calling it a *virtue* makes it look optional; calling it
*insurance* makes it a move you execute regardless of temperament.

**Falsification condition (state it — it's what makes this a bet, not dogma):** the bet is false if a
*longitudinal, randomized study of long-term transferable capacity* shows heavy AI-collaborators do **not**
regress on transfer tasks with no AI present (or improve via redistribution). Leading indicators: the
first multi-year longitudinal datasets appear (currently zero); the long-term gap between structured and
unguided use converges or widens.

## The value of slow — a time constant AI cannot compress

Underwrite the scaffold with the **speed axiom**: the value of certain processes lies precisely in being
**slow**. Memory consolidation needs physical time and sleep (the spacing effect; sleep-stage replay;
Stickgold / Diekelmann & Born, grade II) — time the agent **cannot compress**. Design the scaffold
*along* this time constant (spaced reviews, struggle windows), not against it. "Doing" won't fall to
near-zero cost the way "knowing" did, because what the loop changes is the learner's structure, and
structural change has its own clock independent of parameter count.
