# The scaffold and active resistance — engineering the difficulty in

The scaffold (脚手架) is step ③ of the kernel loop made concrete on the learning surface. The
engineering surface's ③ is "the codebase is queryable, same-source for people and machines." Learning's
③ goes further: the scaffold is **not an answer warehouse but the construction site for rebuilding
cognitive structure** — and uniquely, it must build *difficulty* in, because the surrounding stack
smooths all friction away by default.

## Resist convenience by process, not willpower

The single most important design principle: **do not resist convenience by self-discipline; resist it
by process.** The reason is pragmatic — human willpower is scarce and depletes, while convenience's
default gravity is present 24/7, tireless. In a long contest, willpower loses. So the protocol always
lands on "build the scaffold," not "be more disciplined." This is a structural relocation: move
resistance from the **willpower layer** (refought daily) to the **process layer** (designed once, then
runs automatically). Once "self-answer before asking AI" is hardened into the default route,
resisting convenience stops being an uphill fight and becomes going with the current.

## The error-correction loop (FIG L.4) — where doing actually grows

"Doing" (knowing-how) grows only inside this loop: **attempt → error → self-correct → verify → redo.**
"Error" is not waste, it is signal; "correction" is the moment learning happens. The loop changes the
learner's *cognitive structure*, not the artifact — which is why it can't be done on their behalf
(skill psychology calls the slow compile from "I know the rule" to "I do it without thinking"
*proceduralization*; it has its own time constant, unrelated to model size).

**The agent's only legitimate seat is ④ verify — entered *after* the learner's self-correction.** Move
it ahead of ① to draft for the learner and the whole loop is bypassed: the artifact ships while the
learner's structure stays still. This is the most common way a scaffold quietly becomes a crutch.

## The three pillars (FIG L.5)

1. **Error-and-reflection log.** Records *deviation, not answers* — where the learner was wrong and
   *why*. Welds in the testing effect (active retrieval > rereading for long-term retention; Roediger &
   Karpicke 2006, grade II, replicable). Fields spec'd in `artifacts-spec.md`.
2. **Same-source, diffable knowledge base.** Plain text both learner and agent read and write. The
   value is not "software features" but three underlying principles: *same-source* (no proprietary
   black box between the learner and their own past thinking), *diffable/versioned* (you can see **how**
   your understanding changed), and it collapses the distance between the learner and their cognitive
   trajectory to zero — no mediator they don't control. (Same principle as the engineering surface's
   same-source codebase, in a different instance.)
3. **Deliberately inserted difficulty** — the counter-intuitive pillar, unique to learning. Almost
   every learning tool's default evolution is "smoother, more effortless"; this surface argues the
   reverse. In a convenience-default stack, guardrails (reminders, error-prevention) are not enough —
   the learner must actively *design desirable difficulty into the process*. It lands on three
   concrete substitutions (Bjork's desirable-difficulty family, grade II):
   - replace **presentation** (rereading, being explained to) with **retrieval** (self-testing,
     closed-book redo);
   - replace **massing** (cram it all at once) with **spacing** (spread-out review);
   - replace **blocking** (grind one type) with **interleaving** (mix problem types).

   All three make the **present** harder, slower, less comfortable, yet make **long-term** retention and
   transfer better. The design rule is *against* product intuition: a good scaffold creates friction in
   the right places, it does not eliminate all friction.

## The three resistance postures (active resistance · 主动抵抗)

"Actively resisting convenience" does **not** mean "use AI less / not at all" (asceticism). It means
**changing the order and rhythm** of collaborating with AI so the error-correction loop still has room
to happen. Three concrete, low-cost, immediately actionable postures — all of which *reorder* rather
than *reduce usage*:

1. **Deliberately think before you ask.** For any question headed to the agent, spend ~2 minutes writing
   your own hypothesis/first answer, *then* feed it to AI. This guarantees your cognition takes the
   field first and the agent falls back to the verify seat.
2. **Deliberately do it by hand once.** For a skill you want truly internalized, periodically do it from
   scratch with no AI, no autocomplete — slow, even wrong; this is the only action that leaves a
   "doing"-layer trace.
3. **Deliberately delay help.** At a sticking point, give yourself a fixed struggle window (e.g. 15
   minutes) before consulting AI. That window *is* the desirable difficulty, where the memory trace
   hardens.

**Bjork's boundary:** desirable difficulty benefits only learners with **enough background to respond
successfully**; below that it is merely *undesirable* difficulty (frustration, not learning). Resistance
is measured, not self-flagellation — calibrate the friction to the learner's base.

## Scaffold vs. crutch — and the removal test

Cognitive offloading is itself neutral (the extended-mind thesis: paper, maps, notebooks are all
external brains). What decides scaffold vs. crutch is **not "AI or no AI"** but **where it falls on the
capability-growth curve and which way context flows.** A scaffold's defining feature is that it is
**removable** (Vygotsky [R17]: support withdrawn in steps as ability grows, until the learner stands
alone). A crutch is the opposite: the longer used, the more the supported muscle atrophies. The *same*
AI is a scaffold to someone who derives first and asks it to check, a crutch to someone who asks for the
answer outright and never reproduces it. The difference is the **order and the removal path.**

The break-point (FIG L.9) is **not a usage-frequency threshold** — it is *the moment the removal test
fails.* When the learner can no longer answer "can I still stand if this support is taken away," the
scaffold has already become a crutch without their noticing. This hands resistance an operable wrench:
not "use AI less," but **run periodic removal drills** — every so often, redo the core task with no AI,
treating the removal test as a routine check-up. (This is exactly the dashboard's "transfer" signal.)

## Series seam · trust-but-verify

This surface is isomorphic to the engineering surface's **trust-but-verify**: engineering distrusts AI
*artifacts* and keeps a human verification loop (guarding external correctness); learning distrusts its
own *dependence* and keeps a human cognitive loop (guarding internal capability). Same discipline of
"deliberately retain a human step," on two surfaces. Name this seam when the learner also ships work.
